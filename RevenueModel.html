Insights: Exploring Revenue Models for Social and Non-Social Enterprises with Mr. Karl Dominic Fajardo

By: Krysel April Tiempo

Date: 14/11/24

Reflecting on Mr. Karl Dominic Fajardo’s insights, it becomes clear that the design of revenue models speaks volumes about an organization’s values and purpose. Social and non-social enterprises approach revenue in fundamentally different ways, shaped by whether their primary drive is profit or societal impact.

In social enterprises, the revenue model is much more than a financial strategy; it is an extension of their mission. For instance, the Fee-for-Service Model allows social enterprises to cover operational costs while staying true to their purpose by charging directly for beneficial services. This model reflects a value system where every transaction is a step toward social improvement, creating a sustainable cycle that connects impact with financial viability. Similarly, the Cross-Subsidization Model is a powerful example of inclusivity in action. By charging affluent clients higher rates, these enterprises can provide low-cost services to underserved populations, bridging gaps in access and equity. The structure of these models shows that in social enterprises, profit is a means, not an end—it is the fuel that powers change.

Conversely, non-social enterprises prioritize profit and shareholder value, structuring their revenue models around growth and market dominance. The Subscription-Based Model epitomizes this approach by offering predictable, recurring income, essential for stability and scalability. Customers pay regularly for access, which fosters loyalty and builds long-term revenue streams. This model is efficient, focused on financial stability and user retention. Another popular approach, the Freemium Model, allows companies to attract a wide user base with free access, aiming to convert a portion into paying customers. Though it democratizes initial access, the primary aim remains profit generation, as it serves to maximize reach and potential revenue, not necessarily societal benefit.

Mr. Fajardo’s insights bring to light the role of purpose in revenue design. For social enterprises, revenue is a tool to fulfill a mission, often bending traditional profit motives to align with broader societal goals. Non-social enterprises, however, typically remain centered on profitability, structuring models to maximize income and shareholder returns. This contrast reveals that revenue decisions are not just about financial gain; they reflect the organization’s deeper commitments. Leaders and entrepreneurs can learn from these models, designing revenue streams that align with both financial sustainability and the values that define their organizations.
